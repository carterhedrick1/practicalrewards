Practical Explanation

Every cash back card falls into one of three main types: flat rate, category, or rotating. The type you choose fundamentally changes how you need to think about spending and the mental overhead required.

Flat rate cards give you the same percentage on everything (1.5-2%). Category cards give higher rates (3-6%) on specific types of purchases. Rotating cards offer the highest rates (usually 5%) but only on categories that change every quarter.

Get 1.5-2% back on everything, always. The simplest option with no categories to track, no quarterly activations, just consistent rewards.

Break-Even Analysis: When is a Fee Worth It?

Here's how to figure out if a category card with an annual fee beats a free 2% flat-rate card. Let's use the Blue Cash Preferred ($95 fee, 6% on groceries) as an example.

The BCP earns 4% more on groceries than a 2% card (6% - 2% = 4%). To find the spending needed to cover the $95 fee with that extra 4%, the calculation is:

$$ \text{Break-Even Spending} = \frac{\text{Annual Fee}}{\text{Bonus Reward Rate}} = \frac{\$95}{0.04} = \$2,375 $$

You need to spend $2,375 per year (or about $198 per month) on groceries just to break even with a simple, no-fee 2% card. If you spend more than that, the fee card is better. If you spend less, the no-fee card wins.

Practical Explanation

Points are the "currency" of travel credit cards. But unlike cash back, they don't come with a neat little price tag. That's the trick: banks and airlines get to decide what your points are worth, and the value changes depending on how you use them.

Here's what that looks like in real life:

Standard Value: A $500 flight costs 50,000 points (1 cent per point - this is normal)
Good Value: The same flight might cost 25,000 points (2 cents per point - much better)
Great Value: Or it might only cost 12,500 points (4 cents per point - now you're getting somewhere)

Same flight, three totally different outcomes. With cash back, $500 is always $500. With points, you're either winning big or wondering why you bothered.

Understanding Point Values

Points don't come with price tags. Banks and airlines decide what your points are worth, and that value changes depending on how you use them. Understanding this concept is key to getting the most from your rewards.

What is CPP? CPP stands for "cents per point" - it's how much each point is worth in dollars. This is the standard way to measure point value.
Simple Math: If you have 1,000 points worth 1 CPP, they're worth $10. If you have 50,000 points worth 1 CPP, they're worth $500.
